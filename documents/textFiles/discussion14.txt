On-campus employment and scholarships at Arizona State University (ASU) are realistic options for international students, but availability, eligibility, and competitiveness vary by role and campus. Below is a concise, practical guide to prospects, difficulty, application tips, and realistic expectations.

On-campus job types and who can work

Federal work authorization: International students on F-1 visas may work on campus up to 20 hours/week during fall/spring and full time during official breaks, without additional USCIS authorization. J-1 students follow similar campus-work rules through their sponsor.
Common job categories:
Student worker positions (hourly): department offices, campus libraries, labs, residence life, dining services, athletics, student unions.
Research assistant (RA): often paid hourly or stipend through faculty grants—may prefer grad students but undergrads can get RA roles.
Teaching assistant (TA)/graduate assistant (GA): for graduate students; duties and pay vary by college and are more competitive.
Work-Study: US federal Work-Study funds generally require U.S. financial-need eligibility and are rarely available to international students.
Casual/on-call roles: event staffing, campus tours, exam proctors, technology support.
Student ambassador/brand rep: marketing roles that pay hourly or via stipend; often target undergrads.
Paid internships through campus-affiliated centers: may be available but sometimes limited to U.S. work-authorized students.
How difficult is it to get an on-campus job?

Overall difficulty: moderate. Entry-level hourly roles (library, dining, student employment) are usually obtainable with basic application and interview; demand rises at start of semester. Research/TA/GA roles and well-paid positions are competitive and depend on department funding, qualifications, and timing.
Factors affecting chances:
Visa status: F-1/J-1 are eligible; others may be restricted.
English proficiency and communication skills: many roles require good verbal skills.
Experience and skills: technical roles (IT, lab work, RA) require relevant skills; front-desk and dining need customer service skills.
Timing: hiring surges before semesters and at orientation; fewer openings mid-semester.
Network and faculty relationships: research and TA roles strongly favor students who build rapport with faculty or graduate program coordinators.
Campus location: main Tempe campus has the largest pool of jobs; Polytechnic, Downtown Phoenix and West campuses have fewer roles.
How difficult is it to get scholarships?

Types of scholarships:
Institutional merit scholarships: awarded at admission to many international freshmen/transfer applicants; renewable if GPA criteria met.
Departmental scholarships: for specific colleges (engineering, business, arts) — competitive and based on application or faculty recommendation.
Competitive university scholarships: awards like Barrett Honors College scholarships or specialized awards — selective.
External scholarships: funded by foundations, governments, home-country programs.
Need-based aid for internationals: very limited at ASU; most need-based federal/state funds are for U.S. citizens/permanent residents only.
Difficulty level: variable. Merit scholarships for incoming international students are possible if academic profile is strong (GPA, test scores where required, leadership, essays). Mid-degree scholarships and departmental awards can be competitive and require strong departmental ties or portfolios.
Practical realities:
Incoming international students should apply early and include all required materials; meeting scholarship deadlines is crucial.
Most generous aid goes to top candidates; many international students rely on personal funding plus smaller awards.
Graduate assistantships (TAs/GAs) can effectively subsidize tuition and are often the main source of funding for grad students; these are competitive and tied to departments.
Application tips and tactics

Start early: submit scholarship and job applications before/at the start of the semester. Many positions fill quickly.
Use ASU resources:
ASU Handshake (job portal) for student positions.
Career and Professional Development Services for resume/interview prep.
Departmental webpages and faculty labs for research openings.
International students office for immigration rules and work-authority clarification.
Build network: attend departmental seminars, talk to professors about research needs, join student organizations and residence life programs.
Tailor applications: emphasize relevant skills, class schedule availability, and prior work or volunteer experience.
Be flexible: accept part-time or lower-paid roles initially to build campus references.
For scholarships: prepare strong essays, highlight leadership and accomplishments, provide clear academic records and recommendations, meet renewal criteria (GPA).
Estimated timelines and realistic earnings

Hiring timelines: many hourly positions post 2–6 weeks before semester start; research/RA roles depend on faculty funding cycles.
Typical wages: campus student jobs commonly pay between Arizona minimum wage and modestly above (~$13–$20/hr depending on role and qualifications). Graduate assistantships vary—may include stipend plus tuition benefits.
Work-limitation impact: 20 hours/week cap during semester constrains income—plan budgets accordingly.
Examples and outcomes (typical stories)

Undergraduate international student: secured library assistant job in week one through Handshake, ~10–12 hrs/week, helpful for campus integration and modest income.
International graduate student: obtained GA in department after contacting faculty, responsible for grading and recitations, received stipend and tuition reduction — required demonstrating relevant coursework and communication skills.
Incoming scholarship recipient: awarded institutional merit scholarship at admission due to high GPA and leadership; scholarship covered a portion of tuition and required maintaining a 3.5 GPA to renew.
Bottom line

On-campus jobs at ASU are accessible to international students, especially entry-level hourly roles; research and assistantships are competitive but achievable with proactive networking and strong academic credentials. Scholarships for internationals exist but are more limited and competitive than for domestic students; graduate assistantships are the most reliable path to significant funding for graduate students.
Practical next steps

Create an ASU Handshake profile, check job postings weekly.
Attend career fairs and departmental meetings during the first two weeks of semester.
Contact faculty whose research aligns with your interests and offer to volunteer or assist.
Review ASU admission pages and departmental scholarship deadlines now if applying for incoming-term awards.
